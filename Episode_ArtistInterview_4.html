    Catching up with The Shins in Zurich

    Artist interview
    18:30 | 18 Mar 2013
    JORDAN DAVIS
    WRS JOURNALIST
    audio player here
    SHARE

    Reporter Anna Wirz catches up with The Shins frontman James Mercer at their Zurich Open Air appearance. They speak about their new album, Port of Morrow, touring, sleeping pills and finding those special nuggets that inspire his songwriting.

    Where do you start when you consider the concept of the Swiss in the World?

    Switzerland has been globalised since the Middle Ages, but why? Because Switzerland is an extremely small country, extremely compartmentalized, regionalized: they united in order to safeguard their freedom, in the 13th century. If you have the Gotthard pass, the fastest North-South route of the 13th century, you have the tollbooths, if you have the tollbooths you have the money and you can therefore aspire to liberty. Freedom is linked to the Tell myth of course but also to the fact that technically, the Swiss were able to buy their freedom. With this also came military aggression: it was really a country of barbarians in the early Middle Ages, its the Italians that say so, like Machiavelli for instance. Like a lot of mountain peoples, the Swiss were very warrior-like and they were at war with the Kings of France, the Habsburgs, the Italians, thats all part of Swiss legend and at the same time reality, but after the battle of Marignan (1515) they realized that attacking the King of France every year wasnt feasible. It wasnt even a defeat, it was a retreat: you cant have half of the nations youth (8,000 men) felled in a single battle, so they concluded a perpetual peace treaty with the French. On this basis they made peace and started to export their main asset, war-making, the biggest business in the world.

    This is the root of the tradition of Swiss mercenaries?

    Yes, although they were not technically mercenaries, because there were contractual documents between states. The canton of Uri, for instance, had an agreement with France to provide companies of soldiers. This made Switzerland rich and it also made it part of the globalization process. Everything is beginning to be connected: you start on a private economic basis but you need everyone to be involved, hence the creation of the banks and other networks, Switzerland has had global banks since the Middle Agesand as you also have democratic structures developing, people vote on tax laws, thus taxes are low and money is stable. Foreigners then invest here because they know the money is secure.

    So Switzerlands sometime reputation as insular is a myth? Its more social cohesion than insularity?

    Foreigners often wonder how Switzerland is such a peaceful country but in fact the elites and the masses had the same aims and this military service abroad gave the elite what they needed: education, status, big incomes and internal and external political influence. We are of course not going to claim everything was ideal for the masses but it did mean status and money for them also. Returning soldiers were a good thing as they had learned about the rest of the world, but those who stayed on and built their lives abroad, those who died abroad were also a good thing because they didn’t come back and ask for a job or a house: statistically speaking all this was fantastic for Switzerland. That’s why it could build up this wealth and be quite well off until the 19th century and after the end of military service it could bounce back on other points, industry and so on.
